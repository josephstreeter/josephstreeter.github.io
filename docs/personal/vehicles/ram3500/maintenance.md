## 2014 RAM 3500 Cummins 6.7L Diesel Dually Maintenance Guide

## Overview

This page serves as a maintenance reference for a 2014 RAM 3500 equipped with the Cummins 6.7L turbo diesel engine (ISB 6.7) in a dually (DRW) configuration. It covers fluid capacities, service intervals, and common maintenance procedures.

## Fluid Capacities

| Fluid | Capacity | Specification |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Engine Oil (with filter) | 12 quarts | 15W-40 (CJ-4 rated) or 5W-40 synthetic (CJ-4 rated) |
| Coolant | 24 quarts (6 gallons) | Mopar OAT coolant (purple, MS-12106) |
| Transmission Fluid (68RFE Auto) | 17 quarts (full service) | ATF+4 (MS-9602) |
| Transfer Case (if 4WD) | 2.5 quarts | ATF+4 (MS-9602) |
| Front Differential (if 4WD, AAM 9.25) | 3.5 pints | 75W-90 synthetic (MS-8985) |
| Rear Differential (AAM 11.5) | 4.75 pints | 75W-140 synthetic (MS-8985) |
| Power Steering | Fill to mark | ATF+4 (MS-9602) |
| DEF Tank | ~10 gallons | API certified Diesel Exhaust Fluid (ISO 22241) |
| Diesel Fuel Tank | 52 gallons | Ultra-Low Sulfur Diesel (ULSD, 15 ppm max) |

## Scheduled Maintenance Intervals

### Engine Oil and Filter

- **Interval:** Every 15,000 miles or 6 months (whichever comes first)
- **Severe duty interval:** Every 7,500 miles or 6 months when towing frequently, operating in dusty conditions, or making frequent short trips
- **Oil:** 15W-40 conventional (CJ-4) or 5W-40 full synthetic (CJ-4). Synthetic recommended for cold climates
- **Filter:** Mopar 68157291AA or equivalent Cummins-approved filter
- **Capacity:** 12 quarts with filter change

**Required Tools:**

- 24mm socket or wrench (drain plug)
- Oil filter wrench (cap style for Mopar canister filter)
- Drain pan (minimum 15-quart capacity)
- Torque wrench (inch-pound and foot-pound)
- Funnel with long neck
- Shop rags / absorbent pads
- Nitrile gloves

**Steps:**

1. Warm the engine to operating temperature (180-200°F) to thin the oil for complete drainage. Shut the engine off
2. Position a drain pan under the oil pan. The drain plug is located on the passenger side of the oil pan
3. Remove the 24mm drain plug. Allow oil to drain completely (10-15 minutes)
4. Inspect the drain plug gasket and replace if damaged or deformed
5. Reinstall the drain plug and torque to **37 ft-lbs**
6. Remove the oil filter using the oil filter wrench. The filter is located on the driver side of the engine above the front axle
7. Wipe the filter mounting surface clean with a shop rag. Verify the old filter gasket is not stuck to the mounting surface
8. Apply a thin film of clean engine oil to the new filter gasket
9. Thread the new filter on by hand until the gasket contacts the mounting surface, then tighten an additional 3/4 to 1 full turn by hand (or torque per filter instructions)
10. Add approximately 11.5 quarts of oil through the fill cap on the valve cover using a funnel
11. Start the engine and let it idle for 30-60 seconds. Check for leaks at the drain plug and filter
12. Shut the engine off and wait 5 minutes for oil to settle
13. Check the dipstick and add oil as needed to bring the level to the full mark. Total capacity is 12 quarts with filter
14. Reset the oil life monitor in the EVIC (if applicable) by cycling the ignition and following the on-screen prompts
15. Record the mileage and date in the maintenance log

### Fuel Filter

- **Interval:** Every 15,000 miles
- **Part:** Mopar 68065608AA (or current supersession)
- **Notes:** The 6.7L Cummins uses a single spin-on fuel filter mounted on the driver side of the engine. Drain the water separator at each oil change or when the dashboard indicator illuminates

**Required Tools:**

- Fuel filter wrench (band-style or strap wrench)
- Small drain pan or container
- Shop rags
- Nitrile gloves
- Clean diesel fuel for pre-filling the new filter

**Steps:**

1. Shut the engine off. The fuel filter is a spin-on canister located on the driver side of the engine, near the top
2. Place a drain pan or rags beneath the filter housing to catch fuel spillage
3. Open the water-in-fuel (WIF) drain valve at the bottom of the filter housing and drain any accumulated water into a container. Close the drain valve
4. Using the fuel filter wrench, unscrew and remove the old fuel filter. Some fuel will spill — have shop rags ready
5. Pre-fill the new fuel filter with clean diesel fuel to minimize air in the system and reduce cranking time on restart
6. Apply a thin film of clean diesel or engine oil to the new filter gasket
7. Thread the new filter on by hand until the gasket contacts the housing, then tighten an additional 3/4 turn by hand. Do not over-tighten
8. Cycle the ignition key to the ON position (do not crank) for 30 seconds to allow the lift pump to prime the fuel system. Repeat 3-4 times
9. Start the engine. It may crank longer than normal on the first start. This is normal while air purges from the fuel lines
10. Let the engine idle and inspect the filter for leaks
11. If the engine stumbles or will not start, continue cycling the ignition to prime the lift pump before cranking again

### Air Filter

- **Interval:** Inspect every 15,000 miles; replace every 30,000 miles or as needed
- **Part:** Mopar 53034051AB (or equivalent)
- **Notes:** Replace sooner in dusty environments. Do not use oiled aftermarket filters as they can damage the Mass Airflow sensor

**Required Tools:**

- No tools required (tool-less air box clips on 2014 models)
- Compressed air (optional, for cleaning a reusable filter)
- Shop vacuum (optional, for cleaning the air box)

**Steps:**

1. Locate the air filter housing on the driver side of the engine compartment, near the fender
2. Release the clips on the air filter housing cover (typically 3-4 spring clips)
3. Lift the cover and remove the old air filter element
4. Inspect the air box interior for dirt, debris, leaves, or rodent nesting material. Vacuum out any debris
5. Hold the old filter up to a light source. If you cannot see light passing through the filter media, replace it
6. Insert the new air filter with the rubber seal facing down, ensuring it seats properly in the housing
7. Reinstall the housing cover and secure all clips until they snap firmly into place
8. Verify the intake tube from the air box to the turbo inlet is secure and the clamp is tight

### Coolant

- **Interval:** First change at 97,500 miles or 97 months, then every 30,000 miles or 24 months
- **Type:** Mopar OAT (Organic Acid Technology) coolant — purple color (MS-12106)
- **Notes:** Do not mix OAT coolant with conventional green coolant. Use only distilled water if topping off a 50/50 mix

**Required Tools:**

- 15mm socket or wrench (petcock or lower radiator hose clamp)
- Large drain pan (minimum 7-gallon capacity)
- Funnel or spill-free coolant fill kit
- Distilled water (for flush)
- 6 gallons of pre-mixed Mopar OAT coolant (or 3 gallons concentrate + 3 gallons distilled water)
- Pliers (for hose clamps if needed)
- Coolant test strips or refractometer

**Steps:**

1. Allow the engine to cool completely. Never open the cooling system when hot — pressurized coolant causes severe burns
2. Place a large drain pan underneath the radiator. Open the petcock valve at the bottom of the radiator (driver side) by turning it counterclockwise
3. Remove the radiator cap or surge tank cap to allow air in and speed draining
4. Allow the system to drain completely (10-15 minutes). Approximately 6 gallons will drain
5. Close the petcock valve
6. For a flush: fill the system with distilled water, run the engine to operating temperature with the heater on full heat, shut the engine off, allow it to cool, and drain again. Repeat until the water runs clear
7. Close the petcock valve securely
8. Fill the system slowly through the surge tank with pre-mixed Mopar OAT coolant. Fill slowly to minimize air pockets
9. Leave the surge tank cap off. Start the engine and run it with the heater set to maximum heat. Watch the coolant level in the surge tank — it will drop as air pockets bleed out
10. Top off the coolant as the level drops. Continue running until the thermostat opens (you will feel the upper radiator hose get hot) and the level stabilizes
11. Install the surge tank cap. Let the engine reach full operating temperature and verify the cooling fans cycle on
12. Allow the engine to cool, then recheck the coolant level in the surge tank. Top off to the cold fill line if needed
13. Check for leaks at the petcock, hose clamps, and surge tank
14. Dispose of old coolant properly — it is toxic to animals and must be recycled

### Transmission Service (68RFE)

- **Interval:** Every 60,000 miles under normal conditions; every 30,000 miles under severe duty (frequent towing)
- **Fluid:** ATF+4 only (MS-9602)
- **Notes:** Drop the pan, replace the filter, and refill. A full flush requires approximately 17 quarts. The 68RFE is sensitive to fluid condition — do not exceed service intervals when towing regularly

**Required Tools:**

- 10mm socket (pan bolts)
- T-27 Torx bit (filter bolts on some models)
- Torque wrench
- Drain pan (minimum 6-gallon capacity)
- Gasket scraper or plastic razor blade
- RTV silicone sealant (if pan does not use a reusable gasket)
- Transmission filter kit (includes filter, gasket, and seals)
- 6-8 quarts ATF+4 (pan drop and fill)
- Funnel with small-diameter tube to fit the dipstick tube
- Shop rags
- Nitrile gloves

**Steps:**

1. Warm the transmission by driving for 10-15 minutes. Warm fluid drains more completely
2. Raise the vehicle on a lift or support it securely on jack stands on a level surface
3. Place a large drain pan under the transmission pan
4. Remove the rear pan bolts first, then loosen the front bolts to allow the pan to tilt and drain the fluid in a controlled manner
5. Remove all remaining bolts and lower the pan. Approximately 4-5 quarts will drain
6. Inspect the pan for metal shavings or debris. A small amount of fine gray sediment on the magnet is normal. Chunks or large flakes indicate internal damage — investigate further
7. Clean the pan thoroughly with a lint-free cloth. Clean the magnet
8. Remove the old transmission filter by pulling it straight down from the valve body. Some models use Torx bolts to secure the filter
9. Install the new filter and O-ring (included in the filter kit). Ensure the O-ring seats properly in the valve body bore
10. Scrape the old gasket material from the pan mating surface and the transmission case using a plastic scraper. Do not gouge the aluminum
11. Install the new gasket on the pan (or apply a thin bead of RTV if using a reusable pan without a gasket)
12. Raise the pan into position and hand-start all bolts before tightening to ensure proper alignment
13. Torque pan bolts to **100 inch-lbs** in a criss-cross pattern
14. Lower the vehicle. Add approximately 5 quarts of ATF+4 through the dipstick tube using a funnel
15. Start the engine and shift through all gears (P-R-N-D-3-2-1) pausing 3-5 seconds in each position, then return to Park
16. With the engine idling and the transmission at operating temperature (180°F), check the fluid level on the dipstick. Add fluid as needed to reach the hot full mark
17. Recheck for leaks after a short test drive

### Transfer Case (4WD Models)

- **Interval:** Every 60,000 miles normal; every 30,000 miles severe duty
- **Fluid:** ATF+4 (MS-9602)

**Required Tools:**

- 3/8" drive ratchet (fill and drain plugs are 3/8" square drive on most models)
- Drain pan
- Fluid pump or squeeze bottle for filling
- 2.5 quarts ATF+4
- Shop rags

**Steps:**

1. Raise the vehicle and support securely on jack stands or use a lift
2. Locate the transfer case — it is bolted to the rear of the transmission
3. Place a drain pan beneath the transfer case
4. Remove the **fill plug first** (upper plug on the side of the case) using a 3/8" square drive. Removing the fill plug first confirms you can refill before draining
5. Remove the drain plug (lower plug on the bottom of the case)
6. Allow the fluid to drain completely (5-10 minutes)
7. Inspect the magnetic drain plug for metal debris. Clean it thoroughly
8. Reinstall the drain plug and tighten securely
9. Using a fluid pump or squeeze bottle, fill through the fill plug hole with ATF+4 until fluid begins to seep out of the fill hole (approximately 2.5 quarts)
10. Reinstall the fill plug and tighten securely
11. Wipe up any spilled fluid and check for leaks after a short drive

### Differentials

- **Interval:** Every 60,000 miles normal; every 30,000 miles severe duty
- **Front (AAM 9.25):** 75W-90 synthetic gear oil (MS-8985) — add limited-slip additive if equipped
- **Rear (AAM 11.5):** 75W-140 synthetic gear oil (MS-8985) — add limited-slip additive if equipped
- **Notes:** Dually trucks place additional load on the rear differential. Consider the severe duty interval if regularly towing or hauling heavy loads

**Required Tools:**

- 3/8" square drive or hex socket (fill and drain plugs vary by axle)
- Drain pan
- Fluid pump or large squeeze bottle
- 75W-90 synthetic gear oil for front (AAM 9.25) — approximately 3.5 pints
- 75W-140 synthetic gear oil for rear (AAM 11.5) — approximately 4.75 pints
- Limited-slip friction modifier additive (if equipped with a limited-slip differential)
- Shop rags

**Steps (Rear Differential — AAM 11.5):**

1. Raise the vehicle and support securely on jack stands or a lift
2. Locate the fill plug and drain plug on the differential cover. The fill plug is typically on the side or rear cover; the drain plug is at the bottom
3. Remove the **fill plug first** to confirm it is not seized before draining
4. Place a drain pan beneath the differential and remove the drain plug
5. Allow the fluid to drain completely (5-10 minutes). Inspect the magnetic drain plug for metal particles and clean it
6. Reinstall the drain plug and tighten securely (typical torque: 30-35 ft-lbs)
7. If equipped with a limited-slip differential, add the appropriate friction modifier to the fluid before pumping it in (follow additive instructions for quantity)
8. Using a fluid pump or squeeze bottle, fill through the fill plug hole with 75W-140 synthetic gear oil until fluid seeps out of the fill hole (approximately 4.75 pints)
9. Reinstall the fill plug and tighten securely
10. Repeat the same process for the front differential (AAM 9.25) using 75W-90 synthetic gear oil (approximately 3.5 pints)
11. Inspect both differentials for leaks after a short drive

### Exhaust Brake / Turbo

- **Interval:** Inspect exhaust brake operation periodically
- **Notes:** Ensure the turbo VGT (Variable Geometry Turbocharger) moves freely. Carbon buildup can cause sticking. Short trips without reaching full operating temperature accelerate buildup

**Required Tools:**

- OBD-II scan tool capable of reading Cummins PIDs (e.g., Edge CTS3, DashCommand, or dealer-level scan tool)
- Inspection mirror and flashlight
- Compressed air (optional, for cleaning intercooler tubes)

**Exhaust Brake Inspection Steps:**

1. With the vehicle at operating temperature (180-200°F coolant temp), drive at highway speed and apply the exhaust brake
2. The exhaust brake should provide noticeable engine braking. A weak or absent response indicates a possible issue with the turbo actuator or exhaust brake valve
3. Using an OBD-II scan tool, monitor the VGT actuator position parameter. The VGT vanes should move through their full range when commanded
4. Inspect the exhaust brake/VGT actuator electrical connector for corrosion or damage

**Turbo Inspection Steps:**

1. With the engine off and cool, inspect the turbo inlet boot and all charge air cooler (intercooler) piping for cracks, soft spots, or loose clamps
2. Locate the turbo on the passenger side of the engine. Inspect the oil supply and return lines for leaks or seeping
3. Reach through the intake inlet (with the intake tube removed) and try to wiggle the compressor wheel. Radial play should be minimal — axial (in-out) play should be nearly zero. Excessive play indicates worn turbo bearings
4. Start the engine and listen for unusual whining, rattling, or leaking boost sounds
5. If carbon buildup on VGT vanes is suspected: a VGT cleaning service can sometimes be performed by driving at sustained load (3/4 throttle at highway speed) to heat-cycle the vanes. Severe buildup requires professional cleaning or actuator replacement
6. Check turbo boost pressure with a scan tool under load. Target boost is typically 25-35 PSI under heavy throttle

### Serpentine Belt

- **Interval:** Inspect every 15,000 miles; replace every 100,000 miles or at first sign of cracking
- **Part:** Mopar 53032857AH (or current supersession)
- **Notes:** Also inspect the tensioner and idler pulleys for play or noise at each belt inspection

**Required Tools:**

- 1/2" or 3/8" drive breaker bar or ratchet (belt tensioner)
- Serpentine belt routing diagram (located on the decal under the hood)
- Inspection flashlight
- New serpentine belt

**Steps:**

1. With the engine off, locate the serpentine belt routing diagram on the sticker under the hood or photograph the belt routing before removal
2. Inspect the belt with a flashlight. Look for: cracks or splits across the ribs, missing chunks, glazing (shiny appearance on the rib surface), fraying on the edges, or oil contamination
3. Press on each idler pulley bearing by hand and check for wobble or roughness. Spin each pulley by hand and listen for grinding or rumbling. Replace any pulley that feels rough
4. Check the tensioner for proper spring tension — the tensioner arm should resist movement and snap back firmly when released
5. To remove the belt: insert a 1/2" breaker bar or 3/8" ratchet into the square drive hole on the tensioner and rotate it to relieve belt tension. Slide the belt off a pulley while holding the tensioner
6. Route the new belt following the routing diagram, leaving one pulley (typically the tensioner or an idler) for last
7. Rotate the tensioner to relieve tension, slip the belt over the last pulley, and release the tensioner
8. Verify the belt is fully seated in each pulley groove
9. Start the engine and visually confirm the belt is tracking straight on all pulleys

### Battery

- **Interval:** Test annually (especially before winter)
- **Type:** Two Group 65 batteries in parallel
- **Notes:** Clean terminals and check cable tightness at least twice per year. Diesel engines demand high cranking amps — weak batteries cause hard starts in cold weather

**Required Tools:**

- Battery load tester or digital multimeter
- 10mm wrench or socket (battery terminals)
- Battery terminal brush / wire brush
- Baking soda and water (for corrosion cleaning)
- Battery terminal protector spray or petroleum jelly
- Nitrile gloves and eye protection
- New Group 65 batteries with minimum 750 CCA each (if replacing)

**Steps:**

1. Both batteries are located in the engine compartment — one on each side of the engine
2. With the engine off, move the key away from the ignition. Inspect both battery terminals for white or blue corrosion buildup
3. **Test each battery individually:** Connect the battery load tester to the positive and negative terminals of each battery. Load-test at half the CCA rating for 15 seconds. A healthy battery should hold above 9.6V under load
4. If using a multimeter: a fully charged battery reads 12.6-12.8V at rest (surface charge). Do not test immediately after charging — wait 2 hours
5. To clean corroded terminals: disconnect the negative cable first, then the positive. Mix 1 tablespoon of baking soda in 1 cup of water and pour over the terminals and cable ends. Let it bubble, then scrub with the terminal brush and rinse with clean water. Dry thoroughly
6. Reconnect the positive cable first, then the negative. Torque the 10mm terminal bolts to snug — tight enough to not twist by hand, but do not crack the plastic
7. Apply battery terminal protector spray or a thin coat of petroleum jelly to both terminals to slow future corrosion
8. If replacing batteries: remove the negative cable from both batteries first, then both positive cables, then remove the hold-down brackets. Install one battery at a time. Connect positive cables first, then negative. Check for tight connections

## Diesel Exhaust Fluid (DEF) System

- **DEF consumption:** Approximately 2-5% of fuel consumption (roughly 1 gallon of DEF per 50 gallons of diesel)
- **Tank capacity:** ~10 gallons
- **Notes:** Use only API-certified DEF meeting ISO 22241. Store DEF between 12°F and 86°F. DEF degrades in heat and freezes at 12°F (-11°C). The truck has a heated DEF tank, but avoid storing jugs in direct sunlight or extreme heat

**Required Tools:**

- No special tools required for topping off
- OBD-II scan tool (for fault code diagnosis if DEF warning lights illuminate)
- DEF-compatible funnel or jug with direct-fill spout

**DEF Top-Off Steps:**

1. The DEF filler cap is located near the diesel fuel filler, typically on the driver side rear of the truck. It is blue with a DEF label
2. Open the blue DEF cap. Never use a diesel fuel nozzle in the DEF port — the opening is intentionally smaller to prevent misfueling
3. Pour or pump DEF into the tank. Do not overfill — leave approximately 1 inch of headspace
4. Reinstall the blue cap and ensure it clicks locked
5. If DEF gets on the truck's finish, rinse it off promptly with water. DEF is mildly corrosive to painted surfaces and chrome when allowed to dry

**DEF System Warning Lights:**

| Warning Stage | Indicator | Action |
| --- | --- | --- |
| DEF Low | Amber warning light | Add DEF at next opportunity |
| DEF Very Low | Amber + message | Add DEF immediately |
| DEF Empty | Red warning + engine derate | Add DEF — engine will derate to 5 MPH if ignored |
| DEF Quality Fault | Amber warning | Check DEF for dilution/contamination; replace fluid |

**DEF Quality Check Steps:**

1. If a DEF quality fault code is set, the DEF may have been diluted with water, contaminated, or degraded from heat exposure
2. Drain the DEF tank using a hand pump through the filler neck. Some dealerships have a DEF tank drain procedure
3. Flush the tank with approximately 1 gallon of fresh, certified DEF, then drain again
4. Refill with fresh, certified API DEF (ISO 22241)
5. Clear the DEF quality fault code using an OBD-II scan tool. If the fault returns, the DEF quality sensor may need replacement

## Diesel Particulate Filter (DPF)

- **Passive regeneration:** Occurs automatically during highway driving at sustained speeds
- **Active regeneration:** The ECM initiates a regen cycle when soot load reaches ~80%. Engine idle may increase, and exhaust temps will rise. Do not shut off the engine during an active regen
- **Forced regeneration:** If the DPF warning light illuminates, drive at highway speed for 20-30 minutes. If it does not clear, a dealer-level scan tool may be needed
- **Service interval:** DPF ash cleaning is recommended every 150,000-200,000 miles

**Required Tools:**

- OBD-II scan tool with Cummins-specific DPF parameters (e.g., Edge CTS3, DashCommand, or dealer WiTECH)
- No tools required for normal regen-inducing driving

**Understanding Regen Status:**

1. Monitor DPF soot load percentage and DPF differential pressure using an OBD-II scan tool to understand how often the truck is regenerating
2. Passive regen occurs automatically when exhaust temperatures are high enough (sustained highway driving >200-300 mile trips). No action is needed
3. Active regen is initiated by the ECM. During an active regen, exhaust temperatures can exceed 1,000°F — park away from dry grass, paper, or other flammables. Do not shut the engine off until the regen completes (typically 20-40 minutes)

**If DPF Warning Light Illuminates:**

1. Do not turn the engine off. Drive at 55-65 MPH for 20-30 minutes under moderate load to complete an active regen
2. If the light clears, the regen was successful. No further action is needed
3. If the light does not clear after a prolonged drive, connect an OBD-II scan tool and check the DPF soot load. If soot load is above 90%, a **parked (forced) regen** performed by a shop with WiTECH or equivalent is needed
4. If the truck enters a hard derate (limited to low speed), a forced regen or DPF removal for cleaning may be required

**DPF Ash Cleaning (150,000-200,000 miles):**

1. Ash is the non-combustible residue from engine oil additives and cannot be burned off by regeneration
2. The DPF must be removed from the vehicle and professionally cleaned using a DPF cleaning machine (pneumatic pulse or aqueous wash process)
3. The truck should be pressure-tested after cleaning to verify the DPF is not cracked

## Tire Maintenance (Dually Specific)

| Position | Pressure (Typical Loaded) | Rotation Pattern |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Front (2 tires) | 60-80 PSI (check door placard) | Front to rear inner |
| Rear Inner (2 tires) | 60-80 PSI (check door placard) | Rear inner to front |
| Rear Outer (2 tires) | 60-80 PSI (check door placard) | Rear outer to rear inner |

- **Rotation interval:** Every 7,500 miles
- **Notes:** Dually tire rotation follows a six-tire pattern. Check pressures cold. Ensure inner and outer rear tires are matched in diameter — mismatched tires cause excessive wear and driveline stress. Inspect inner duals regularly for debris and damage since they are difficult to see
- **Valve stem extenders:** Inspect for leaks at every pressure check. Replace if cracked or corroded
- **Lug nut torque:** 140 ft-lbs for steel wheels; re-torque after 50-100 miles if removed

**Required Tools:**

- Tire pressure gauge (digital recommended; must read up to 100 PSI)
- Floor jack (rated for the truck's GVWR)
- 2 or more jack stands rated for at least 7,000 lbs per side
- 22mm socket (lug nuts)
- Torque wrench capable of 140 ft-lbs
- Wheel chocks
- Breaker bar (lug nut removal)
- Valve core tool (for valve stem extenders)

**Pressure Check Steps:**

1. Check pressures **cold** (before driving more than 1 mile)
2. Remove the valve stem extender cap on each rear inner tire. Inspect the extender for cracks, corrosion, or air seeping from the joint
3. Check each tire's pressure and compare to the door placard specification (typically 65-80 PSI for the rear depending on load rating and load)
4. Add or release air to reach the correct pressure
5. Reinstall valve stem extender caps finger-tight. Do not over-tighten plastic caps

**Tire Rotation Steps (Six-Tire Dually Pattern):**

1. Park on a level surface and apply the parking brake. Place wheel chocks on the tires remaining on the ground
2. Loosen all lug nuts on the wheels being removed before raising the vehicle
3. Raise one end of the truck at a time using a floor jack placed on the frame rail. Support with jack stands
4. **Rotation pattern:**
   - Left front → Left rear inner
   - Left rear inner → Left rear outer
   - Left rear outer → Left front
   - Repeat mirror-image for the right side
5. When reinstalling rear dual wheels, torque the inner wheel lug nuts first, then install the outer wheel over the same studs and torque the outer lug nuts
6. Torque all lug nuts to **140 ft-lbs** in a star pattern
7. Lower the vehicle and re-torque lug nuts after driving 50-100 miles

## Grease Points

- **Interval:** Every 7,500 miles or at each oil change
- **Points:** Front U-joints (if equipped with grease fittings), front axle u-joints (4WD), driveshaft slip yoke, ball joints, tie rod ends, drag link ends
- **Grease type:** NLGI #2 multi-purpose lithium grease
- **Notes:** The 2014 RAM 3500 has multiple grease zerks on the front end. Neglecting lubrication causes premature wear in steering and suspension components, especially under heavy loads

**Required Tools:**

- Grease gun (pistol-grip or lever-action)
- Grease cartridge (NLGI #2 multi-purpose lithium grease)
- Needle-nose grease coupler (for hard-to-reach zerks)
- Rags or paper towels
- Inspection light or flashlight
- Floor jack and jack stands (optional, improves access)

**Grease Zerk Locations and Steps:**

1. Raise the vehicle and support on jack stands for better access to all grease points, or access from beneath with the vehicle on the ground
2. Wipe each grease zerk clean before attaching the grease gun coupler to prevent pushing dirt into the joint
3. Connect the grease gun coupler firmly over each zerk fitting. Pump slowly until you see fresh grease purging from the edge of the boot or joint seal. Stop immediately when grease purges — over-greasing blows out the boot
4. **Grease points to service:**
   - Front upper and lower ball joints (1 zerk each) — both left and right
   - Tie rod ends (inner and outer) — both left and right
   - Drag link ends (both ends)
   - Front track bar ends (if equipped with zerks)
   - Front driveshaft slip yoke (1 zerk)
   - Front driveshaft U-joints (2 zerks per U-joint, 2 U-joints) — 4WD models
   - Rear driveshaft slip yoke (1 zerk)
   - Rear driveshaft U-joints (2 zerks per U-joint, 2-3 U-joints depending on configuration)
   - Steering shaft U-joints (between the steering column and steering box)
5. After greasing, wipe away any purged grease with a rag to prevent it from attracting dirt and dust
6. Check each zerk for damage or blockage. A blocked zerk (one that will not accept grease) should be replaced with a new zerk fitting using a zerk installation tool or a wrench

## Brake System

- **Pad inspection:** Every 15,000 miles or at each tire rotation
- **Fluid:** DOT 3 or DOT 4 brake fluid
- **Fluid change:** Every 2-3 years or when fluid appears dark or contaminated
- **Notes:** Dually trucks are heavy — brake components wear faster under frequent towing. Inspect rotors for scoring and measure thickness at each pad inspection. Rear drums (if equipped) should be inspected every 30,000 miles

**Required Tools:**

- Floor jack and jack stands
- 21mm socket (lug nuts)
- Torque wrench
- Brake pad thickness gauge or ruler
- C-clamp or brake piston wind-back tool (for rear calipers if applicable)
- Digital micrometer (for rotor thickness)
- Wire brush (for cleaning caliper slide pins)
- Brake cleaner spray
- Synthetic caliper slide pin grease (not petroleum-based)
- Brake fluid (DOT 3 or DOT 4)
- Turkey baster or brake fluid vacuum pump (for fluid changes)
- Clear tubing and collection bottle (for bleeding)

**Brake Pad and Rotor Inspection Steps:**

1. Raise the vehicle and remove the wheels
2. Inspect the brake pad thickness through the caliper inspection window or by looking at the outer pad from the side. Minimum pad thickness is typically **2-3mm (1/8 inch)**. Replace if at or below minimum
3. Inspect the brake rotor surface for:
   - Deep grooves or scoring — replace if depth exceeds 0.030"
   - Blue heat discoloration (indicates overheating from dragging caliper or extended towing)
   - Lateral runout (wobble) — can be measured with a dial indicator
   - Measure rotor thickness with a micrometer and compare to the minimum thickness spec stamped on the rotor edge
4. Inspect the caliper slide pins for free movement. Remove the pins, clean off old grease with brake cleaner, and apply fresh synthetic slide pin grease. Stiff or corroded slide pins cause uneven pad wear and dragging brakes
5. Inspect brake hoses for cracking, swelling, or abrasion

**Brake Fluid Change Steps:**

1. With the engine off, locate the brake fluid reservoir on the master cylinder (driver side firewall)
2. Use a turkey baster or vacuum pump to remove old fluid from the reservoir. Fill with fresh DOT 3 or DOT 4 fluid
3. Starting at the wheel farthest from the master cylinder (typically right rear), attach clear tubing to the bleeder screw and run it into a collection bottle
4. Open the bleeder screw 1/4 to 1/2 turn. Have an assistant slowly press the brake pedal fully and hold it. Close the bleeder screw before the pedal hits the floor. Release the pedal. Repeat until fluid coming out of the bleeder is clear and free of bubbles
5. Keep the master cylinder reservoir topped off throughout the process — do not let it run dry or air will enter the system
6. Repeat at the remaining wheels in order: right rear, left rear, right front, left front
7. Top off the reservoir to the MAX line and inspect for leaks at all bleeders

## Seasonal Maintenance Tips

### Cold Weather

**Required Items:**

- Engine block heater cord (verify the truck has a working block heater at the front of the engine)
- 5W-40 full synthetic CJ-4 engine oil
- Diesel anti-gel additive (e.g., Power Service Diesel 911 or similar)
- Battery load tester
- Distilled water and hydrometer (coolant concentration check)

**Steps:**

1. Switch to 5W-40 full synthetic CJ-4 engine oil before temperatures drop below 20°F — it flows significantly better at cold temperatures than 15W-40 conventional
2. Locate the block heater cord at the front of the engine and plug it into a standard 110V outlet for a minimum of 2-4 hours before starting in temperatures below 0°F. This dramatically reduces wear on cold starts and improves starting reliability
3. Test both batteries with a load tester before the first hard freeze. Replace any battery below 750 CCA or that fails the load test
4. Verify coolant concentration with a hydrometer or refractometer. The 50/50 Mopar OAT mix protects to approximately -34°F. If you are in an extreme cold climate, do not exceed 70% coolant concentration
5. Begin adding diesel anti-gel additive to each fuel fill-up when temperatures drop to 20°F or below. Follow the dosing instructions on the product. Cloud point of standard diesel is around 15-20°F; anti-gel lowers the point at which wax crystals form and block the fuel filter
6. If the fuel filter becomes gelled (hard start or no start in cold): use a diesel emergeny defrost product and allow it to work for 20-30 minutes, or move the vehicle to a warm garage before attempting to start
7. Do not top off the DEF tank above 70% capacity before long cold storage — DEF expands as it freezes. The tank heater handles normal conditions, but this reduces stress on the tank during prolonged extreme cold

### Hot Weather

**Required Items:**

- Coolant temperature gauge or OBD-II scan tool for monitoring
- Garden hose (for cleaning radiator and intercooler fins)
- Compressed air or soft bristle brush (for cleaning fins)

**Steps:**

1. Inspect the radiator, A/C condenser, and charge air cooler (intercooler) fins for bugs, dirt, and debris. Use a garden hose from the engine side (rear) of the radiator/condenser stack to spray debris outward through the front. Compressed air works for lighter buildup
2. Damaged or bent cooling fins can be carefully straightened with a fin comb tool
3. When towing in hot weather, monitor coolant temperature with a scan tool or EVIC gauge. If coolant temp climbs above 220°F, reduce load, increase speed slightly to improve airflow, or pull over and idle in park (the cooling fans will run)
4. Do not shut the engine off immediately after hard towing in hot weather — idle for 3-5 minutes to allow coolant to continue circulating and cool the turbocharger bearing housing
5. Store DEF containers in a shaded location. DEF stored above 86°F for extended periods degrades and will cause quality fault codes when added to the tank
