## 2019 Heartland Fuel M-352 Maintenance Guide

## Overview

This page is a maintenance reference for the 2019 Heartland Fuel Series M-352 fifth-wheel toy hauler. Maintenance intervals are based on calendar time and usage since RVs are not driven continuously like motor vehicles. Inspection before and after each trip is critical for safety on the road.

> [!WARNING]
> Always chock the wheels and engage the parking brake before working under or around the trailer. Never work under a trailer supported only by landing gear or stabilizer jacks — use properly rated jack stands.

| Interval | Service Items |
| --- | --- |
| Before each trip | Tire pressure and condition, lug nut torque check, hitch and kingpin inspection, brake controller test, all lights, slide-out seals, roof visual, LP detector and CO detector test |

| Every 3 months / each season | Lubricate slide-out mechanisms, inspect and lube landing gear, check battery state of charge |

| Every 2-3 years | Replace roof lap sealant (full application), replace slide-out wiper seals if deteriorated |
| Seasonal | Winterization (before first freeze), de-winterization (before first use in spring) |

## Pre-Trip Inspection

**Required Tools:**

- Tire pressure gauge (must read to 110 PSI — ST235/80R16 Load Range E max is 80 PSI cold)
- Torque wrench (lug nuts)
- Flashlight / inspection mirror
- Test light or multimeter (for trailer lights)

**Steps:**

1. **Tires:** Check all four tires cold with a gauge. Recommended pressure for ST235/80R16 Load Range E is typically **65-80 PSI** — verify on the tire sidewall. Inspect sidewalls for cracks, bulges, or cuts. Check tread depth
2. **Lug nuts:** Torque all lug nuts before each trip departure if any wheel was removed. Torque to **120 ft-lbs** for the 16-inch aluminum wheels (verify with your wheel specifications). Check visually for any that appear loose
3. **Kingpin and hitch:** Inspect the kingpin for cracks, wear, or deformation. Verify the fifth-wheel hitch jaw is fully locked and the safety latch is engaged before moving
4. **Safety chains / breakaway:** Confirm safety cables are connected and the breakaway cable is attached to the tow vehicle separately from the safety chains
5. **Brake controller:** Connect the trailer, verify the brake controller shows trailer connection, and manually apply the brake controller to confirm trailer brakes engage (you should feel the trailer slow when manually triggered at low speed)
6. **Lights:** With a helper or by using a light tester, verify all running lights, brake lights, turn signals, and reverse lights function
7. **Landing gear:** Verify the landing gear is fully retracted and the manual crank handle is stowed. Landing gear contacting the ground at highway speed causes catastrophic damage
8. **Stabilizer jacks:** Verify all stabilizer jacks are fully retracted
9. **Slide-outs:** Confirm all slides are fully retracted and latched
10. **Roof-mounted items:** Verify A/C units, satellite dishes, or antennas are not extended or raised
11. **Ramp door:** Confirm the rear ramp door is fully closed and latched. Check that the ramp lock/travel latch is secured
12. **Cargo:** Verify all interior cargo is secured. Shifted cargo can change the pin weight distribution mid-trip

## Every 3 Months / Each Season

### Slide-Out Mechanism Lubrication

- **Type:** White lithium grease spray for rack-and-pinion or cable systems; petroleum-free slide-out lubricant for rubber seals

**Required Tools:**

- White lithium grease spray can
- Slide-out rubber seal conditioner (303 Aerospace Protectant or dedicated RV slide-out seal lubricant)
- Rags

**Steps:**

1. With the slides extended, locate the rack-and-pinion gear rail or cable/pulley system along the top and bottom of each slide mechanism (visible from inside the trailer)
2. Apply white lithium grease along the full length of the gear rack on both sides of each slide
3. Extend and retract the slide several times to distribute the lubricant
4. Wipe down the rubber wiper seals around each slide with a clean rag
5. Apply slide-out seal conditioner (do NOT use petroleum-based lubricant on rubber seals — it accelerates deterioration) to keep the seals pliable and prevent cracking
6. Inspect the seals for tears, compression set, or gaps. A slide that does not seal in the rain or allows visible light at the edges needs new seals

### Landing Gear and Stabilizer Jack Lubrication

**Required Tools:**

- White lithium grease spray
- Penetrating oil (for corrosion)
- Cordless drill (for faster operation when lubricating)

**Steps:**

1. Manually extend and retract the landing gear partially to expose the screw mechanism
2. Spray white lithium grease on the exposed screw thread and gearbox input shaft
3. Cycle the landing gear several times to work grease into the mechanism
4. Lubricate each stabilizer jack's screw mechanism with white lithium grease
5. Inspect for bent legs, stripped gears, or worn motor brushes. Landing gear that hesitates or grinds warrants further inspection

### Battery Maintenance

**Required Tools:**

- Digital multimeter
- Battery hydrometer (for flooded lead-acid batteries)
- Battery terminal brush
- Baking soda and water (for corrosion)
- Battery maintainer / trickle charger (for storage)

**Steps:**

1. Locate the two house batteries (typically in an external battery compartment on the tongue or driver-side front)
2. Check the state of charge with a multimeter: 12.6-12.8V = full charge; 12.2V = 50% discharged; below 12.0V = discharged (lead-acid). Recharge if below 12.4V
3. For flooded lead-acid batteries: check the electrolyte level in each cell. Top off with **distilled water only** to just above the plates. Do not overfill
4. Inspect terminals for corrosion. Clean with baking soda and water, rinse, dry, and apply terminal protector spray
5. If the trailer will be stored for more than 2 weeks without shore power, connect a battery maintainer/trickle charger or disconnect the negative battery cable to prevent parasitic drain from LP detectors, CO detectors, and the converter

## Every 6 Months / Annually

### Roof Inspection and Reseal

> [!IMPORTANT]
> The roof is the most critical maintenance area on any RV. Water infiltration through failed sealants causes hidden structural damage that can cost tens of thousands of dollars to repair. Inspect and reseal at least once per year.

**Required Tools:**

- Soft-soled shoes (rubber-soled only — no hard heels)
- Dicor self-leveling lap sealant (for flat roof penetrations and seams)
- Dicor non-sag lap sealant (for vertical surfaces and sidewalls)
- Plastic putty knife / caulk tool
- Mineral spirits or denatured alcohol (cleaner)
- Rags
- Caulk gun

**Steps:**

1. Access the roof using a sturdy ladder. Walk only on the roof structure above the walls and above structural ribs — avoid the center of unsupported spans
2. Inspect the entire roof surface (rubber EPDM or TPO membrane) for punctures, tears, blisters, or bubbles. Press on any bubble — if it moves, there is delamination or water below
3. Inspect all roof penetrations: A/C units, vents, antennas, solar panels, the front cap seam, and the rear cap seam. Look for cracking, shrinking, pulling away, or gaps in the sealant
4. Remove any loose or cracked sealant with a plastic putty knife. Do not gouge the roof membrane
5. Clean the area thoroughly with mineral spirits or denatured alcohol and allow to dry completely
6. Apply Dicor self-leveling lap sealant to all horizontal/flat penetrations and seams. Apply generously — it will self-level and flow into gaps
7. Apply Dicor non-sag sealant to any vertical seams, the front and rear cap seams, and sidewall trim where it meets the roof
8. Inspect the A/C mounting gaskets. Push gently on the A/C unit — it should not rock. If it does, the mounting gasket has compressed and the unit needs to be re-sealed with a new roof gasket
9. Allow all sealant to cure for 24-48 hours before rain exposure

### Wheel Bearing Inspection and Repack

- **Interval:** Annually or every 12,000 miles, whichever comes first
- **Grease type:** NLGI #2 marine/trailer bearing grease (or wheel bearing grease rated for trailer use)

> [!NOTE]
> Bearing failure is a leading cause of trailer fires and highway incidents. Do not skip this service.

**Required Tools:**

- Floor jack and jack stands
- Lug wrench (115mm / 4.5 in socket for the dust cap; specialty cap puller recommended)
- Pliers (cotter pin removal)
- Flat-head screwdriver and hammer (dust cap removal if no puller)
- Bearing packer tool or zip-lock bag method
- Torque wrench
- Wheel bearing grease
- Clean rags and parts-cleaning solvent
- Replacement cotter pins, castle nuts (have spares)
- Replacement bearing seals

**Steps:**

1. Chock the wheels that will remain on the ground. Raise one side and place on jack stands
2. Remove the wheel and set aside
3. Remove the dust cap using a cap puller or by gently tapping around the circumference with a screwdriver and hammer
4. Straighten and remove the cotter pin. Discard — never reuse cotter pins
5. Remove the castle nut and thrust washer
6. Slide the hub toward you slightly to release the outer bearing. Catch it — it will fall
7. Pull the hub off the spindle. The inner bearing and grease seal are in the back of the hub
8. Use a seal driver or large socket to drive the old grease seal out of the hub
9. Remove the inner bearing
10. Clean all bearing races, bearings, and the hub cavity thoroughly with solvent. Inspect the bearing races (the metal rings pressed into the hub) for pitting, scoring, or discoloration from heat. Replace the entire hub if the races are damaged
11. Inspect each bearing roller and cage for pitting, flat spots, or rust. Replace bearings as a set if any damage is found
12. **Repack the bearings:** Place a golf-ball-sized amount of grease in your palm. Press the wide end of the bearing cage into the grease and work it in around all rollers until grease purges out the other side. Repeat until fully packed. Or use a bearing packer tool
13. Install the inner bearing into the hub. Tap a new grease seal in with a seal driver or wood block until flush
14. Slide the hub onto the spindle. Install the outer bearing, thrust washer, and castle nut
15. Torque the castle nut to **75 ft-lbs** to seat the bearings, then back off 1/4 turn. Advance until the next cotter pin hole aligns — the nut should be snug (25-30 ft-lbs maximum) with zero hub end-play. The hub should spin freely with no drag
16. Install a new cotter pin and bend the legs over the nut
17. Pack the dust cap with a small amount of grease and tap it back into place
18. Reinstall the wheel and torque lug nuts to **120 ft-lbs** in a star pattern
19. Re-torque lug nuts after driving 50 miles

### Electric Brake Inspection and Adjustment

- **Interval:** Annually or whenever braking feels uneven or weak

**Required Tools:**

- Wheel removed (from bearing repack step above)
- Brake drum puller (if drums are stuck)
- Brake shoe gauge or ruler
- Brake cleaner spray
- Flathead screwdriver (adjuster wheel)

**Steps:**

1. With the wheel removed, inspect the brake drum interior for scoring, heat cracks, or excessive wear. Measure drum diameter — replace if beyond the wear limit stamped on the drum
2. Inspect the brake shoes for lining thickness (minimum 1/16 in). Replace shoes as a set (both shoes on both drums of the same axle) if worn
3. Inspect the brake magnet (the electromagnet inside the drum) for wear — it should have flat grooves worn by the drum. Replace if worn more than halfway through
4. Clean all brake components with brake cleaner
5. Adjust the brake shoes: use a flathead screwdriver to rotate the star adjuster wheel (accessible through the slot in the backplate) until the shoes just contact the drum, then back off 5-8 clicks until the drum spins freely with slight drag
6. Reinstall the wheel and repeat on all four wheels
7. After reassembly, verify the brake controller output is calibrated: at 25 MPH, apply the brakes and manually trigger the brake controller gain test. The trailer should brake firmly without lockup

### Water Heater Anode Rod

- **Interval:** Annually (LP/electric combination water heaters with an anode rod)
- **Anode rod size:** Standard RV anode rod (usually 1 in NPT, 4 in or 9 in length — verify for your unit)

**Required Tools:**

- 1-1/16 in socket or water heater rod socket (specialty)
- Drain pan and hose
- Teflon tape

**Steps:**

1. Turn off the water heater (LP and electric switch off). Allow the tank to cool completely
2. Connect a garden hose to the drain fitting or open the pressure relief valve to relieve pressure first
3. Remove the anode rod from the side of the water heater using the 1-1/16 in socket. The rod will have accumulated white/grey corrosion deposits — this means it is doing its job
4. Inspect the rod: if it is less than 25% of its original diameter, replace it
5. Wrap the threads of the new anode rod with 2-3 layers of Teflon tape
6. Install the new rod and snug it firmly (do not over-torque — water heater tanks are aluminum)
7. Refill the water heater by opening a hot water faucet inside and turning on the water pump until a steady stream (no sputtering) flows. Close the faucet
8. Turn the water heater back on

### Generator Service (Onan 5.5kW LP)

- **Interval:** Every 150 hours or annually
- **Refer to:** Onan generator operator's manual for complete service specifications

**Required Tools:**

- Spark plug socket (5/8 in)
- Air filter (Cummins Onan 140-3280 or equivalent)
- Spark plugs (Onan specified — Champion or equivalent)
- Torque wrench
- Voltmeter (to check generator output voltage)

**Steps:**

1. Run the generator for 5 minutes to warm it up, then shut it off. Allow it to cool before servicing
2. Locate the generator in the external compartment (typically pass-through or driver-side front)
3. Remove the generator housing access panels
4. **Air filter:** Remove the air filter cover and inspect or replace the foam/paper filter element
5. **Spark plugs:** Remove the spark plugs with the spark plug socket. Inspect: light grey/tan color is normal. Black = rich mixture; white = lean. Replace with new plugs and torque to **12-15 ft-lbs**
6. **Exercise regularly:** Run the generator under at least 50% load for 2 hours per month minimum. Generators that sit unused develop carburetor varnish and moist cylinders
7. **Check output voltage:** With a voltmeter at an internal outlet, the generator should output 120V ±5% at rated load. Low voltage indicates governor or load issues
8. **LP fuel supply:** Verify the LP regulator output pressure is within spec. Low LP pressure causes surging and low output

## Seasonal Maintenance

### Winterization

> [!IMPORTANT]
> Failure to properly winterize will result in burst water lines, cracked pump housings, failed valves, and water heater damage when temperatures drop below 32°F.

**Required Supplies:**

- RV non-toxic antifreeze (pink, propylene glycol) — typically 3-4 gallons
- Compressed air with blow-out plug (1/2 in MPT fitting to air compressor quick-connect)
- Water pump bypass kit (if not already installed)
- Flathead screwdriver

**Compressed Air Method (Preferred):**

1. Drain the fresh water tank completely by opening the drain petcock or hose
2. Drain the water heater: turn off the heater, relieve the pressure at the relief valve, and remove the drain plug at the bottom of the heater. Allow to drain fully
3. Open all hot and cold faucets (kitchen, bathroom, outdoor shower) and the toilet flush valve — leave them open
4. Connect the blow-out plug to the city water inlet (external water connection)
5. Connect the air compressor. Set regulator to **30-50 PSI maximum** (higher pressure can damage pump diaphragm and plastic fittings)
6. Starting with the faucet closest to the pump, hold the faucet open until air has run through (5-10 seconds), then close it. Move to the next faucet in sequence. End with the toilet
7. Open and blow out the outdoor shower if equipped
8. After completing all outlets, remove the blow-out plug and add approximately 1 cup of RV antifreeze down each drain trap (p-trap under sinks and the toilet) to prevent trap freeze
9. Pour 1-2 cups of antifreeze into the toilet bowl through the flush valve
10. Close the water heater bypass valve (bypass the water heater so it does not fill with antifreeze)

**Antifreeze Method (Backup / for low-point drains that won't blow clear):**

1. Install a water pump bypass kit if not equipped
2. Connect a length of tubing from the water pump inlet to a jug of RV antifreeze
3. Open each faucet one at a time until pink antifreeze flows, then close
4. Flush the toilet until pink antifreeze appears in the bowl

**Additional Winterization Steps:**

1. Lubricate all door locks and moving parts with WD-40 or white lithium spray before storage
2. Cover vent openings with covers or mesh to prevent rodent entry
3. Place rodent-deterrent packs or dryer sheets inside the trailer and in the garage
4. Remove all food, propane-powered appliances (if needed), and perishables
5. Disconnect the battery negative cable or connect a battery maintainer if stored where power is available
6. Cover the trailer with a breathable RV cover if stored outdoors, or store in a covered facility

### De-Winterization (Spring Preparation)

**Required Supplies:**

- Fresh potable water
- Water system sanitizer (RV-specific, bleach-based or commercial)
- Water filter cartridge (inline, if equipped)

**Steps:**

1. Remove all rodent deterrents and check for any evidence of rodent intrusion — chewed wires, insulation damage, or nesting
2. Remove any vent covers installed for winter storage
3. Reconnect the negative battery terminal and verify the battery charge. Put on a charger if needed
4. Open the water heater bypass valve to allow the tank to fill
5. Connect to shore water or use the onboard pump with fresh water in the tank. Open all faucets and flush completely to clear antifreeze. Run until water runs clear at each outlet
6. **Sanitize the water system:** Mix 1/4 cup of household bleach per 15 gallons of fresh water tank capacity. Fill the tank with this solution, then open each faucet and run until you smell bleach. Close the faucets, let the solution sit for 4-12 hours, then drain completely. Flush with 2-3 fresh water fills until there is no bleach odor
7. Replace any inline water filter cartridges
8. Fill the fresh water tank with potable water and verify the water pump operates correctly
9. Test all LP appliances: range, water heater, furnace, and refrigerator (if LP mode)
10. Inspect all roof sealants and slide-out seals before the first trip
11. Perform the tire inspection, bearing check, and brake check per the annual maintenance steps

## Onboard Fuel Station Maintenance

- **Interval:** Before each use season; service pump annually if used regularly

**Steps:**

1. Inspect the fuel station fill cap and vent for debris or blockage
2. If the fuel in the station tank has been sitting for more than 30 days without a fuel stabilizer, add a dose of fuel stabilizer (STA-BIL or equivalent) at the beginning of the season
3. If fuel has been sitting more than 6 months, drain the old fuel and refill with fresh fuel
4. Test the 12V transfer pump: turn on the pump switch and verify fuel flows through the nozzle. Listen for the pump to run smoothly without cavitation (stuttering)
5. Inspect the fuel hose and nozzle for cracks, kinks, or fuel odor indicating a leak
