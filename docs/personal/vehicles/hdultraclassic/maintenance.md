## 2007 Harley-Davidson Ultra Classic Maintenance Guide

## Overview

This page is a maintenance reference for the 2007 Harley-Davidson Ultra Classic Electra Glide (FLHTCU) equipped with the Twin Cam 96B engine. Service intervals follow Harley-Davidson's recommended schedule based on mileage. The motorcycle uses three separate fluid sumps — engine oil, primary chaincase, and transmission — each requiring its own fluid type.

> [!WARNING]
> Always place the motorcycle on the jiffy stand or a center stand on firm, level ground before performing maintenance. Ensure the engine is off and the key is removed. Allow the engine to cool before draining hot oil.

## Fluid Capacities

| Fluid | Capacity | Specification |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Engine Oil (with filter) | 3.5 quarts | Harley-Davidson SYN3 Full Synthetic or equivalent JASO MA-rated 20W-50 motorcycle oil |
| Primary Chaincase | 32 oz (1 quart) | Harley-Davidson Formula+ Primary Chaincase Lubricant (or JASO MA-rated 80W gear oil) |
| Transmission | 24 oz (3/4 quart) | Harley-Davidson SYN3 transmission fluid or JASO MA-rated 75W-90 gear oil |
| Fork Oil (each leg) | 11.4 oz (340 ml) | Harley-Davidson Type E Fork Oil (or equivalent 10W fork oil) |
| Brake Fluid (front and rear) | Fill to MAX line | DOT 4 |
| Fuel Tank | 6.0 gallons (including reserve) | Regular unleaded (87 AKI minimum) |

> [!NOTE]
> Harley-Davidson SYN3 is a single fluid marketed for engine, primary, and transmission use. It meets the JASO MA specification. Do not use automotive engine oil (API SN or later with friction modifiers) — these will cause clutch slipping.

## Service Intervals at a Glance

| Interval | Service Items |
| --- | --- |
| Before each ride | Tire pressure, fluid levels (engine oil), lights, throttle free-play, brake lever feel, drive belt tension and condition |
| Every 5,000 miles | Engine oil and filter, primary chaincase fluid, transmission fluid, check and adjust drive belt tension |
| Every 10,000 miles | Spark plugs, air filter, fuel filter (inline), brake fluid inspection, throttle and clutch cable lubrication |
| Every 20,000 miles | Fork oil, brake fluid replacement, fuel filter replacement |
| Annually (or before storage) | Battery inspection, brake caliper pin lubrication, tire inspection for age/cracking, electrical connector inspection |

## Pre-Ride Inspection

**Steps:**

1. **Engine oil:** Check level at the dipstick on the right side of the engine (under the seat). Engine must be warm and the bike on level ground on the jiffy stand. Level should be between the ADD and FULL marks. Do not overfill — overfilling causes oil to be pushed past seals and into the air cleaner
2. **Tire pressure:** Front: **36 PSI**; Rear: **36 PSI** (unloaded solo). Check cold. The correct pressure for two-up or loaded riding may differ — refer to the tire sidewall and owner's manual
3. **Drive belt:** Inspect the belt for cracks, fraying, glazing, or missing teeth. Check tension per the procedure below
4. **Brakes:** Squeeze the front brake lever and press the rear brake pedal — both should feel firm with no sponginess. Check brake fluid level in both reservoirs
5. **Lights:** Verify headlight, taillight, brake light (front and rear activation), and turn signals
6. **Throttle:** Roll the throttle open and release — it should snap back to closed freely with no sticking. Check at full steering lock both directions

## Every 5,000 Miles

### Engine Oil and Filter

- **Interval:** Every 5,000 miles or annually, whichever comes first
- **Oil:** Harley-Davidson SYN3 Full Synthetic 20W-50 (or JASO MA-rated 20W-50 motorcycle oil). Use SYN3 or equivalent 60W in extreme heat, 10W-40 in very cold climates
- **Filter:** Harley-Davidson 63798-99A (chrome) or 63731-99A (black) — or equivalent
- **Capacity:** 3.5 quarts with filter change

**Required Tools:**

- 5/8 in socket or oil filter wrench (drain plug — some models use a drain bolt, others a hex plug)
- Oil filter wrench (76mm 14-flute cap or strap wrench)
- Drain pan
- Torque wrench
- Funnel
- Shop rags
- Nitrile gloves

**Steps:**

1. Run the engine for 5 minutes to warm the oil. Shut off and allow 2-3 minutes for oil to settle in the sump
2. Place a drain pan under the right lower side of the engine. The oil drain plug (or hex drain) is at the bottom of the oil pan
3. Remove the drain plug/bolt and allow the oil to drain fully (5-10 minutes)
4. Inspect and replace the drain plug O-ring or sealing washer if flattened or damaged
5. Reinstall the drain plug and torque to **14-21 ft-lbs** (oil drain plug)
6. Move the drain pan under the oil filter — it is located on the right forward side of the engine
7. Remove the oil filter with a filter wrench. Wipe the mounting surface clean
8. Apply a thin film of fresh engine oil to the new filter gasket
9. Thread the new filter on by hand until the gasket seats, then tighten an additional 1/2 to 3/4 turn by hand
10. Add 3 quarts of fresh oil through the dipstick tube using a funnel. Start the engine and let it idle for 30 seconds. Shut off, wait 2 minutes, and check the dipstick
11. Add oil in small increments to bring the level to the FULL mark. Do not exceed — total capacity with filter is 3.5 quarts but fill to the dipstick, not to total capacity
12. Start the engine again, check for leaks at the drain plug and filter, and verify the oil pressure indicator extinguishes within 5 seconds

### Primary Chaincase Fluid

- **Interval:** Every 5,000 miles
- **Fluid:** Harley-Davidson Formula+ Primary Chaincase Lubricant or JASO MA-rated equivalent
- **Capacity:** 32 oz (fill to the bottom of the diaphragm cover opening)

**Required Tools:**

- T27 Torx bit (derby cover screws)
- T40 Torx bit or 1/4 in hex (primary drain plug, varies)
- Drain pan
- Funnel or squeeze bottle
- Torque wrench

**Steps:**

1. Place a drain pan under the primary chaincase (left side of motor, behind the footboard)
2. Remove the six T27 Torx derby cover screws and remove the derby cover. Set the derby cover O-ring aside — inspect it and replace if cracked or deformed
3. Locate the primary drain plug at the lowest point of the primary chaincase (bottom left of the bike). Remove the plug with the appropriate tool and allow fluid to drain completely
4. While draining, inspect the primary chain through the derby cover opening for wear, stretch, or dark sludge deposits. The primary chain should have approximately 5/8 to 7/8 in of up-and-down play measured at the mid-point
5. Reinstall the drain plug and tighten securely
6. Using a funnel or squeeze bottle, add 32 oz of primary chaincase fluid through the derby cover opening
7. Verify the fluid level is at the bottom edge of the diaphragm cover bore (the inner step inside the case), or as specified in the service manual
8. Install the derby cover O-ring (lubricate lightly with fresh primary fluid). Reinstall the derby cover and tighten the six T27 screws to **84-108 inch-lbs** (7-9 ft-lbs) in a star pattern

### Transmission Fluid

- **Interval:** Every 5,000 miles
- **Fluid:** Harley-Davidson SYN3 or equivalent JASO MA-rated 75W-90 gear oil
- **Capacity:** 24 oz

**Required Tools:**

- T40 Torx bit (fill plug) and T40 or 5/8 in socket (drain plug)
- Drain pan
- Fluid pump or squeeze bottle
- Torque wrench

**Steps:**

1. Place a drain pan under the transmission (right rear of the engine, under the belt guard)
2. Remove the transmission fill plug (right side of transmission case, mid-height)
3. Remove the drain plug (bottom of transmission case) and allow fluid to drain completely
4. Reinstall the drain plug — torque to **14-21 ft-lbs**
5. Add 24 oz of transmission fluid through the fill plug hole using a pump or squeeze bottle
6. With the motorcycle on level ground on the jiffy stand, fluid should be level with the bottom of the fill plug hole when full
7. Reinstall the fill plug and torque to **14-21 ft-lbs**

### Drive Belt Tension and Inspection

**Required Tools:**

- Drive belt tension gauge (Gates Carbon Drive gauge or H-D special tool) or ruler
- Flashlight

**Steps:**

1. Place the motorcycle on level ground on the jiffy stand. Do not check tension on a center stand
2. Locate the rear drive belt between the transmission sprocket and the rear wheel sprocket
3. Lift the belt at the midpoint with moderate finger pressure (approximately 10 lbs). The deflection should be **5/16 to 3/8 in** at the bottom run. Correct belt tension is critical — too loose and the belt can jump; too tight causes premature bearing wear
4. If adjustment is needed: loosen the rear axle nut and the adjuster locknuts on both sides, then turn the adjuster bolts equally on both sides to move the wheel forward (tighten belt) or back (loosen belt). Recheck tension and verify the wheel is tracking straight
5. Torque the rear axle nut to **95 ft-lbs** after adjustment
6. Inspect the belt for cracks in the teeth, missing teeth, cracking on the back surface, edge fraying, or embedded debris. Replace if any damage is found
7. Spin the rear wheel and watch the belt track through the rear sprocket — it should track centered without wandering to either edge

## Every 10,000 Miles

### Spark Plugs

- **Interval:** Every 10,000 miles
- **Type:** Harley-Davidson 6R12 (or equivalent — NGK DCPR7E, Champion RA8HC)
- **Gap:** 0.038-0.043 in (0.97-1.09 mm)
- **Quantity:** 2 (one per cylinder)

**Required Tools:**

- 5/8 in spark plug socket with rubber insert
- 3/8 in ratchet with 6 in extension
- Torque wrench
- Spark plug gap tool
- Dielectric grease

**Steps:**

1. Allow the engine to cool completely
2. Remove the left and right spark plug wires by gripping the rubber boot and twisting gently while pulling straight off the plug
3. Use the spark plug socket and extension to remove each plug. Inspect:
   - Light grey/tan = normal combustion
   - Black, sooty = rich mixture or oil fouling
   - White/blistered = lean mixture or overheating
4. Check the gap on the new plugs with a gap tool. Set to **0.038-0.043 in** if needed. Do not adjust iridium or platinum plugs — use standard-tip plugs on the Harley 96
5. Apply a small amount of anti-seize to the threads of each new plug (or verify plugs are factory pre-coated)
6. Thread each plug in by hand to avoid cross-threading into the aluminum head
7. Torque to **18-22 ft-lbs** (dry) or **11-15 ft-lbs** (with anti-seize)
8. Apply dielectric grease to the inside of each spark plug boot
9. Press each wire boot firmly onto the plug until it clicks

### Air Cleaner Filter

- **Interval:** Inspect every 5,000 miles; replace every 10,000 miles or when visibly loaded with dirt

**Required Tools:**

- T27 Torx bit (air cleaner cover screws)
- Replacement air filter element (H-D 29700009 or K&N equivalent)

**Steps:**

1. Remove the two T27 Torx screws securing the air cleaner cover (Screamin' Eagle-style cover or stock cover)
2. Remove the cover and the filter element inside
3. Inspect the element: if you cannot see light through it or it is caked with dirt, replace it
4. Wipe the inside of the air cleaner housing with a damp cloth to remove dust
5. Install the new filter element and reinstall the cover

### Throttle and Clutch Cable Lubrication

**Required Tools:**

- Cable luber tool (or improvised with a zip-tie and bag)
- Cable lubricant (H-D cable lubricant or dry PTFE cable lube)

**Steps:**

1. Locate the upper ends of the throttle cables at the throttle housing (right handlebar)
2. Disconnect the cable end from the throttle housing carefully, following the service manual for your specific cable routing
3. Attach the cable luber to the upper end of the cable and inject cable lubricant until it flows out the lower end
4. Repeat for the clutch cable (left side, from the clutch lever to the primary cover)
5. Check cable free play after reassembly: throttle should have 1/16 to 1/8 in play; clutch should engage approximately 1/4 in from the lever fully released

## Every 20,000 Miles

### Fork Oil

- **Interval:** Every 20,000 miles or every 2-3 years
- **Type:** Harley-Davidson Type E Fork Oil or equivalent 10W fork oil
- **Capacity:** 11.4 oz (340 ml) per leg

**Required Tools:**

- 3/4 in socket or wrench (upper fork cap)
- Drain pan
- Fork oil measuring container
- Torque wrench

**Steps:**

1. Support the front of the motorcycle so the front wheel is off the ground (use a front stand or lift the frame under the engine)
2. Remove the front fender if needed for clearance
3. For each fork leg: remove the upper fork cap (3/4 in socket). The fork spring will be under slight tension — keep downward pressure while unscrewing the cap
4. Using long needle-nose pliers or a fork spring puller, remove the fork spring. Note the spring orientation (tapered end up or down per service manual)
5. Pump the fork leg several times to push old oil up, then drain into a pan
6. Allow to drain fully (10-15 minutes)
7. Measure 11.4 oz of fresh fork oil and add to the leg through the upper opening
8. Reinstall the fork spring in the correct orientation
9. Reinstall the fork cap and torque to **10-20 ft-lbs**
10. Repeat for the other leg

### Brake Fluid Replacement

- **Interval:** Every 2 years or when fluid is dark brown, contaminated, or has absorbed moisture
- **Type:** DOT 4

**Required Tools:**

- Clear tubing and collection bottle
- 8mm wrench (bleeder screws)
- Turkey baster or vacuum pump (reservoir extraction)
- Fresh DOT 4 brake fluid
- Nitrile gloves (DOT 4 damages paint — work carefully)

**Steps:**

1. **Front brake:** Locate the master cylinder reservoir on the right handlebar. Remove the cover screws, remove the cover and diaphragm. Extract old fluid with a turkey baster
2. Fill the reservoir with fresh DOT 4 fluid
3. Attach clear tubing to the front brake caliper bleeder screw(s). Open the bleeder 1/4 turn and have an assistant squeeze the front brake lever slowly and hold it. Close the bleeder before releasing the lever. Repeat until clear, bubble-free fluid flows out
4. Keep the reservoir topped off throughout — do not allow it to run dry
5. Fill to the MAX line and reinstall the diaphragm and cover. Torque the cover screws carefully — they are small and the reservoir is plastic
6. **Rear brake:** Repeat the same procedure at the rear master cylinder (located on the right side of the frame below the master cylinder under the right saddlebag) and the rear caliper bleeder

## Annual Maintenance

### Battery Inspection and Service

- **Type:** 12V 19Ah AGM (YTX20L-BS equivalent)
- **Location:** Under the seat, on the left side behind the airbox

**Required Tools:**

- Battery load tester or digital multimeter
- 5/16 in nut driver or socket (battery terminals)
- Battery terminal brush
- Battery maintainer for storage (Battery Tender Plus or equivalent)

**Steps:**

1. Remove the seat (1/4-turn fastener on the seat latch)
2. Disconnect the negative (black) cable first, then the positive (red) cable
3. Remove the battery from its tray
4. Load-test the battery at half its CCA rating for 15 seconds — it should hold above 9.6V
5. Check the battery case for swelling, cracks, or corrosion on terminals. Replace an AGM battery that is swollen
6. Clean the terminal contacts and cable ends with a terminal brush
7. Reinstall the battery. Connect positive first, then negative. Tighten 5/16 in terminal bolts snugly
8. If storing for winter: connect a smart battery maintainer (Battery Tender Plus or equivalent). Do not use a standard trickle charger on an AGM battery

### Brake Caliper Pin Lubrication

**Required Tools:**

- Appropriate socket or wrench to remove caliper (varies)
- Small flathead screwdriver or pick (slide pin removal)
- Brake caliper slide pin grease (not petroleum-based)
- Brake cleaner spray

**Steps:**

1. Remove each brake caliper from its mounting bracket (front and rear)
2. Slide out the caliper guide pins and inspect for corrosion, scoring, or rubber boot tears
3. Clean the pins and their bores with brake cleaner
4. Apply a thin coat of caliper slide pin grease (synthetic, non-petroleum) to the pin shank. Do not grease the rubber boot area
5. Reinstall the pins and calipers. If a pin spins freely without binding through the full travel, it is correctly lubricated
6. Inspect the brake pad friction material through the caliper while it is off the bike. Replace if at or below 1/16 in thickness

### Electrical Connector Inspection

**Steps:**

1. Inspect the major multi-pin connectors on the fairing harness (visible by removing the fairing lower trim pieces)
2. Look for green corrosion, white powdery deposits, or melted insulation at connectors
3. Disconnect each suspect connector and apply dielectric grease to both mating sides before reconnecting
4. Inspect all lighting connections (headlight, turn signals, taillight) at the bulb sockets
5. Check the main battery positive cable connection at the starter and the positive bus, and the negative cable at its frame ground point — both should be clean and tight

## Storage Preparation

**Steps:**

1. Fill the fuel tank completely to prevent condensation inside the tank. Add a dose of fuel stabilizer (STA-BIL or equivalent) and run the engine for 10 minutes to circulate it through the fuel system
2. Change the engine oil and filter before storage — used oil contains acids that attack internal metal surfaces over winter
3. Drain and change the primary chaincase and transmission fluids if the interval is near
4. Check tire pressure and inflate to the maximum sidewall pressure for storage to reduce flat-spotting. Prop the bike flat with a stand under each tire if possible, or move it periodically
5. Remove the battery and connect to a smart maintainer. Place in a warm, dry location
6. Place the motorcycle on the jiffy stand on a firm surface. Slide a piece of wood or rubber pad under the jiffy stand foot if storing on bare concrete
7. Cover with a breathable motorcycle cover
8. Apply a light coat of ACF-50 or similar corrosion inhibitor to all exposed metal, chrome, and electrical connectors
